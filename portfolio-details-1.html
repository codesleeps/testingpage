Monocouche Rendering

  • The render is applied in a single layer directly onto the prepared substrate. Depending on the chosen technique, it can be applied0 by hand using trowels or by using a mechanical sprayer. The thickness of the render layer is controlled to ensure an even and uniform appearance.
  • Monocouche render can be finished in various ways to achieve different textures and appearances. Common textures include scraped, dragged, stippled, or rolled. These textures are created while the render is still wet, giving the façade its final appearance. The chosen texture can influence the visual aesthetics of the building.
  • Once the monocouche render is applied and textured, it needs time to set and cure. This involves a gradual drying process where the render hardens and gains strength. Curing is crucial for the render's long-term durability and weather resistance. The curing time can vary depending on factors like weather conditions and the render formula.
  • Monocouche rendering is popular because it offers a relatively quick and efficient way to finish external walls. It provides a clean and consistent look to buildings while also offering weather protection and thermal benefits. The term "monocouche" itself translates to "single layer" in French, highlighting the key feature of this rendering system - its ability to create a finished appearance in just one application.

Waterproof Rendering

  • Waterproof rendering serves as a barrier against rainwater, humidity, and other forms of moisture, helping to maintain the structural integrity of the building and prevent issues like dampness, mold growth, and erosion.
  • A primer or bonding agent is often applied to the surface before rendering. This helps the rendering material adhere better to the substrate and provides a consistent base.
  • A waterproof rendering mix is prepared using materials such as cement, sand, additives, and sometimes polymer-based compounds. These additives improve the water resistance and flexibility of the rendering.
  • Depending on the desired aesthetic and protective properties, the rendered surface can be finished with various textures, patterns, and colours. Different finishing techniques can be used to achieve the desired appearance.

Rendering Granite and Silicone Finish

  • Applying a suitable primer helps enhance the adhesion of the rendering materials to the surface, ensuring a strong bond. A base coat is applied to create a smooth and consistent substrate for the subsequent layers. This layer helps level the surface and provides a good base for the textured finish.
  • Granite-Coat: This layer is a specially formulated render that contains particles resembling granite or stone. It's applied over the base coat to create the appearance of natural stone. The granular texture adds depth and a visually appealing aspect to the surface.
  • Silicone Coat: The final layer consists of a silicone-based render. Silicone renders are known for their water repellency and breathability, which prevent moisture penetration while allowing any trapped moisture to escape. This layer provides the waterproofing and durability aspects.
  • The silicone render can be tinted to the desired colour, and the texture can be customized as well. This allows for a wide range of design possibilities, mimicking the appearance of natural stone surfaces.

Venetian Plastering

  • Venetian plaster is a wall and ceiling finish consisting of plaster mixed with marble dust, applied with a spatula or trowel in thin, multiple layers, which are then burnished to create a smooth surface with the illusion of depth and texture.
  • Venetian plaster techniques include marmorino, scagliola, and sgraffito. When left un-burnished, Venetian plaster has a matte finish that is rough and stone-like to the touch. Un-burnished Venetian plaster is also very brittle and damages rather easily.
  • When applied correctly, Venetian plaster can be used to create a highly polished, rock-hard, marble-like finish. Venetian plaster is especially useful on surfaces where marble panels could not be installed easily, and on surfaces that would be too expensive to have carved from real marble such as columns, corbels, and curved walls.
  • Polished plastering is a technique that requires practice and patience to master.

Venetian Plastering

  • Apply a primer or base coat that is suitable for polished plaster. This coat helps the plaster adhere properly and promotes an even finish. Mix the base coat plaster material according to the manufacturer's instructions. The base coat is usually tinted to a colour similar to the final desired hue.
  • Apply the base coat to the prepared surface using a trowel. The thickness of the base coat can vary but is typically around 1-2 millimeters. Trowel the base coat to achieve a smooth and even surface. Allow it to dry thoroughly.
  • After achieving the desired polish, apply a protective sealer designed for polished plaster. The sealer enhances the plaster's durability and resistance to moisture. Buff the surface gently with a soft cloth to enhance the shine and create a luxurious appearance.

Venetian Plastering - Stucco - Monocouche

  • Mix the brown coat stucco material, which is a slightly different mixture from the scratch coat. It's also applied at a thickness of about 3/8 to 1/2 inch. Apply the brown coat over the scratch coat and shape it to the desired contours. This coat provides the structural strength and helps to achieve a smooth, level surface. Use a trowel and other tools to ensure the brown coat is even and properly aligned.
  • The finish coat is the final layer and provides the decorative appearance of the stucco surface. Mix the finish coat stucco material, which is often a finer mix of sand, Portland cement, and sometimes additives for colour or texture.
  • Apply the finish coat to the brown coat using a trowel. Depending on the desired finish, you can achieve various textures, from smooth to rough. To create texture, you can use different tools like trowels, sponges, brushes, or even a float to achieve the desired pattern.
  • Stucco needs to cure slowly to prevent cracking. Mist the surface with water regularly for a few days to ensure proper curing. Protect the freshly plastered stucco from extreme weather conditions during the curing process.
  • Burnishing and Polishing: Once the plaster is dry, it's time to achieve the characteristic polished finish. Use a trowel or a specialized burnishing tool to smooth and compact the surface.
  • This process compresses the layers and brings out the plaster's natural luster. Gradually increase the pressure while burnishing to create a high-gloss or satin finish, depending on your preference.

Traditional Pebble Dash

  • A base coat of render is applied to the wall. This provides an even surface for the pebbles to adhere to and helps to level the wall. While the base coat is still wet, small pebbles or aggregates are thrown onto the surface, embedding them partially into the render. The pebbles adhere to the wet render, creating a textured finish.
  • After the pebbles are applied, a skilled plasterer will gently press them into the render to ensure proper adhesion and a consistent texture. The pebble-dash needs time to cure and dry. During this time, it becomes securely attached to the base coat.
  • Depending on the desired finish, the pebble-dash can be left in its natural state or painted to achieve a specific colour scheme. Sealing the pebble-dash with a weather-resistant coating can enhance its durability.
  • Overall, traditional pebble-dash rendering offers a textured and visually appealing finish that not only enhances the appearance of buildings but also provides a durable protective layer against the elements.
